MEME ECONOMY

MEMES AND VISUAL CULTURE

Early Internet Memes – The term ‘meme’ was created by genealogist Richard Dawkins to describe the way that cultural information spreads in society similarly to genes. In the early days of the internet, forum sites like 4chan, Ebaumsworld, Something Awful, etc. began circulating humorous media content between users, who would each add and elaborate. As these pieces of media changed hands they evolved as cultural tokens within the communities that used them. These cultural artefacts were hence dubbed ‘internet memes’. Early internet memes included the peanut butter jelly time dancing banana, nyan cat, rickrolling, and many more.

Photoshop Memes – These memes can be seen as a response to the loss of the authenticity of the digital image. The advent of digital photography and advanced editing software has given media creators the ability to manipulate images without evidence. As a result, photography, which was once a standard of authenticity, is no longer a trustworthy form of media. This issue is reflected in Photoshop memes which range from sardonic humor – a smiling Leonardo DiCaprio juxtapositionally strutting through violent or dangerous scenes – to challenges of inaccurate reporting – clearly photoshopped propaganda images of state leaders mockingly photoshopped into ridiculous situations.

Dank / Deep Fried Memes – As the popularity of image macro memes reached saturation, many internet communities began to lament their overuse. Dank memes were a series of memes that directly commented on this oversaturation with ironically absurd images that commented on users’ obsession with “browsing dank memes”. The ironic format of this genre carried over into deep fried memes which further commented on the redundancy of meme genres by distorting popular meme images to the point of being unrecognizable. The ironic meme genre is also a commentary on the state of media in the post-internet era. The distortion of images as they bounce around the internet becomes a major theme in deep fried memes as the degradation of their meaning is analogous to the degradation of the memes’ meaning.

Photo Fad Memes – In 2011, an explosion of “planking” photographs were posted to internet forums and social media platforms, showing user lying flat on the ground or bridging objects. Later followed by similar trends like “owling”, “batmanning”, etc. These memes began circulating heavily after an Australian Rugby player began performing the plank as his scoring celebration – undoubtedly an imitation of earlier, more isolated photo fads. Photo Fads are indicative of changes in visual culture regarding modern image consumption. Popular poses have existed as long as portraiture itself, but as portrait images become increasingly ubiquitous via social media, the image itself takes precedence, rendering the human subject and their posing unimportant. Photo fad participators ridicule the endless self-documentation that is social media by contributing to a wave of intentionally inane and pointless photos.

Image Macros – Perhaps the most recognizable meme format, the image macro featured heavily on internet forums and social media from around 2007 onward. Its popularity was partially fueled by its consistent format and ease of imitation, featuring only an image and two lines of text on the top and bottom, inspiring a wave of meme generator sites. The image macro creates a stereotypical character out of a stock image and invites users to make a witty observation about them. The popular meme format could be seen as a response to the saturation of digital images that has occurred over the past few decades, particularly stock imagery, which seeks to condense human characteristics to archetypal, searchable pictures, often for the purpose of advertising.
